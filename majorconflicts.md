### I. The World Wars

These two conflicts are the defining events of the century, reshaping global politics, technology, and society.

#### 1. World War I (1914–1918)
*   **Key Participants:** The Allies (primarily Britain, France, Russia, Italy, and later the United States) vs. the Central Powers (primarily Germany, Austria-Hungary, the Ottoman Empire).
*   **Causes:** A complex web of alliances, imperial competition, nationalism, and militarism, triggered by the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand of Austria-Hungary.
*   **Significance:** Known as "The Great War" and later "The War to End All Wars," it introduced brutal industrial-scale warfare, including trench warfare, machine guns, poison gas, and tanks. It led to the collapse of the German, Austro-Hungarian, Ottoman, and Russian Empires and set the stage for World War II.

#### 2. World War II (1939–1945)
*   **Key Participants:** The Allies (primarily Britain, the Soviet Union, the United States, and China) vs. the Axis Powers (primarily Nazi Germany, Fascist Italy, and Imperial Japan).
*   **Causes:** The unresolved issues of WWI, the rise of aggressive, expansionist ideologies (Nazism, Fascism, and Japanese militarism), and the failure of international diplomacy.
*   **Significance:** The most destructive conflict in history, with an estimated 70-85 million fatalities. It saw the Holocaust, the widespread use of aerial bombing, and the first (and only) use of nuclear weapons. It resulted in the decline of European colonial powers, the rise of the United States and the Soviet Union as superpowers, and the beginning of the Cold War.

### II. The Cold War (c. 1947–1991)

A state of geopolitical tension between the two superpowers (USA and USSR) that did not result in direct "hot" war between them but fueled numerous devastating **proxy wars** around the globe.

#### 3. The Korean War (1950–1953)
*   **Key Participants:** North Korea (supported by China and the USSR) vs. South Korea and a United Nations coalition led by the United States.
*   **Significance:** The first major armed conflict of the Cold War. It solidified the division of Korea, demonstrated the policy of "containment" against communism, and was the first "limited war" where the superpowers avoided all-out conflict for fear of nuclear escalation.

#### 4. The Vietnam War (c. 1955–1975)
*   **Key Participants:** North Vietnam (supported by the USSR and China) and the Viet Cong vs. South Vietnam and the United States.
*   **Significance:** A long and divisive conflict, especially for the United States. It was a guerrilla war fought in a dense jungle environment, and its extensive media coverage eroded public support at home. The North's victory in 1975 resulted in the unification of Vietnam under a communist government.

#### 5. The Soviet-Afghan War (1979–1989)
*   **Key Participants:** The Soviet Union and the Afghan government vs. the Afghan Mujahideen (rebels supported by the United States, Pakistan, and others).
*   **Significance:** Often called the "Soviet Union's Vietnam," it was a costly and demoralizing quagmire that drained Soviet resources and morale. The eventual Soviet withdrawal is seen as a major contributing factor to the collapse of the USSR itself. The power vacuum left behind contributed to the rise of the Taliban and al-Qaeda.

#### 6. The Arab-Israeli Conflicts (1948–Present, with major wars in '48, '56, '67, '73)
*   **Key Participants:** Israel vs. various coalitions of Arab states (primarily Egypt, Jordan, Syria, and others).
*   **Significance:** A central and ongoing conflict rooted in competing nationalist and religious claims to the land of historical Palestine. The 1967 Six-Day War was particularly consequential, as it resulted in Israel's occupation of the West Bank, Gaza Strip, Golan Heights, and Sinai Peninsula, the realities of which continue to define the region today.

### III. Major Decolonization & Regional Conflicts

As European empires crumbled, numerous wars of independence and internal conflicts erupted.

#### 7. The Chinese Civil War (c. 1927–1949)
*   **Key Participants:** The Chinese Communist Party, led by Mao Zedong, vs. the Nationalist Kuomintang (KMT), led by Chiang Kai-shek.
*   **Significance:** The fighting was interrupted by the Second Sino-Japanese War (part of WWII) but resumed with a vengeance. The Communist victory in 1949 resulted in the establishment of the People's Republic of China, fundamentally altering the global balance of power. The KMT retreated to Taiwan, creating the long-standing cross-strait issue.

#### 8. The Algerian War of Independence (1954–1962)
*   **Key Participants:** Algerian nationalists (the FLN) vs. France.
*   **Significance:** A brutal and deeply divisive war for France. It involved guerrilla warfare, terrorism by both sides, and widespread torture. Algerian independence marked a major turning point in the decolonization of Africa and led to the collapse of France's Fourth Republic.

#### 9. The Nigerian Civil War (Biafran War) (1967–1970)
*   **Key Participants:** The government of Nigeria vs. the secessionist state of Biafra.
*   **Significance:** A conflict born from ethnic, political, and economic tensions. It is notorious for the famine that resulted from a federal blockade of the Biafran region, creating a humanitarian crisis that was one of the first to be televised globally.

#### 10. The Iran-Iraq War (1980–1988)
*   **Key Participants:** Iran vs. Iraq.
*   **Significance:** One of the longest conventional wars of the 20th century. Fought over territorial disputes and regional dominance, it was characterized by trench warfare reminiscent of WWI, the widespread use of chemical weapons by Iraq, and attacks on civilian oil tankers ("the Tanker War"). It ended in a stalemate with massive casualties and economic devastation for both nations.

#### 11. The Yugoslav Wars (1991–2001)
*   **Key Participants:** The various former republics of Yugoslavia (Slovenia, Croatia, Bosnia and Herzegovina) against the Serb-dominated Yugoslav army and later, Serb forces.
*   **Significance:** A series of brutal ethnically-based conflicts that followed the breakup of Yugoslavia. The war in Bosnia (1992–1995) was particularly notorious for the siege of Sarajevo and the Srebrenica genocide. These wars marked a violent return of ethnic conflict to Europe and prompted a series of interventions by NATO and the UN.
